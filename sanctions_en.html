Sanctions and Financial Restrictions
October 2025

Sanctions used to be described as an “extraordinary measure” — something deployed in rare, extreme cases. That framing is gone. Sanctions are now part of day-to-day international governance. They are not a headline anymore. They are routine.

This shift matters. When sanctions become routine, markets adapt to them. Banks adapt. Payment companies adapt. States adapt. Workarounds emerge, and over time those workarounds stop being workarounds and become parallel infrastructure.

On paper, sanctions are supposed to isolate and apply pressure: cut off access, freeze assets, restrict settlement, complicate trade. That logic assumes there is a single system — one global dollar-based rails network that everyone needs access to and can be excluded from.

But that is less and less true. We are moving away from a single center toward a set of overlapping, partially interoperable systems. Dollar clearing still dominates, yes. But local liquidity pools, regional settlement arrangements, non-dollar corridors, and commodity-based offsets all exist. They are imperfect, but they exist.

As a result, broad sanctions today often behave more like friction than like a wall. They increase cost and complexity. They slow you down. They make compliance harder. They do not always stop activity — especially if the activity is strategically important to both sides.

There is also fatigue. When sanctions are used constantly, including for signaling and domestic politics, the signal weakens. Receiving countries no longer hear: “this is exceptional pressure and we must reconsider.” They hear: “this is normal, we were expecting this, let's activate Plan B.”

None of this is to say sanctions are meaningless. They absolutely can work, especially if they are narrow, specific, and coordinated with financial infrastructure. What is fading is the belief that sanctions are a single decisive instrument. Increasingly, they are one tool among many — and not always the most precise one.
