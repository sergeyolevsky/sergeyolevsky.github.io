Bad Data Is Not a Mistake. It Is a System
April 2026

Have you ever wondered why executives — in companies, banks, and even governments — are so fond of “beautiful numbers”?

Clean charts. Clear metrics. Forecasts that align perfectly.

Everything looks right.

And yet, very often — everything underneath is wrong.

All management is built on data.

Planning. Incentives. Strategy.

But what happens when the data does not reflect reality?

It is not necessarily completely false. Not necessarily fabricated.

Just: adjusted, filtered, aligned with expectations.

Made to look “correct”.

It usually starts small.

Take a supermarket.

The system assumes: items are in stock, order picking takes a fixed amount of time, processes are linear.

But in reality: items may be missing, search takes longer, failures occur.

So employees begin to “adapt”.

They manipulate logins. Adjust time. Correct the data.

Not because they want to cheat.

But because otherwise the system does not work.

Management looks at the reports.

Everything seems fine: metrics are within range, efficiency is visible, no major issues.

But in reality: inventory does not match, processes break, time is lost.

And the most dangerous part — the system believes its own data.

Now scale it.

Supermarket → company → government.

Some collect the data. Others analyze it. Others make decisions.

At each stage, there is: filtering, distortion, the desire to “improve the picture”.

At some point, reality disappears.

At the top level, this becomes political.

Leaders receive not reality, but: a convenient version, aligned expectations, data that does not create problems.

As a result, decisions are made not on facts, but on their interpretation.

And when reality hits — the consequences can be catastrophic.

The main problem is that distorted data does not remain a local issue.

It multiplies.

One flaw requires another, one assumption leads to the next, one mistake is covered by another.

Eventually, the entire system operates within self-deception.

And at some point, it becomes impossible to fix without breaking everything.

Those who pay are not the ones who designed the KPIs. Not those who built the reports. Not those who approved the system.

Those who pay are: people on the ground, operations, middle management, ultimately — the entire company or country.

They are forced to operate within a system that does not reflect reality.

This is not a technological problem.

Not a software issue. Not an analytics issue.

It is structural: systems are designed by those who do not use them.

And those who use them are forced to adjust reality to fit the model.

Bad data is not an accident.

It is the result of: incentives, hierarchy, fear, distance from reality.

And the most dangerous moment is not when the data becomes wrong.

But when everyone agrees to pretend that it is right.
