Concentrate Buying Point as a First Entry into Nigeria
March 2026

When entering Nigeria in the segment of critical minerals such as niobium and tantalum, the key question is not only access to resources, but also choosing the right entry point.

Developing a fully owned mine is a separate model that requires significant investment, time, and operational management.

There is a faster and more manageable approach: setting up a concentrate buying point and working with the raw material flow.

This model allows you to enter the market, understand how it actually works, and start generating revenue.

The structure is simple: buy concentrate locally and sell it further along the chain.

The economics are based on the spread between the purchase price and the resale price.

At the level of artisanal miners, extraction is manual, resources are limited, and cash is needed immediately.

As a result, a simple rule applies: whoever pays fast gets the material.

A buying point does not always need to be located directly next to a specific mining site.

In practice, land control issues, local community dynamics, and overall manageability of the environment can create additional risks.

Location is therefore not just about logistics — it is about control.

A practical implementation of this model is a buying point in Jos, Plateau State.

Its core functions include purchasing niobium and tantalum concentrate, quality analysis, weighing and grading, immediate payment (cash, bank transfer, or cheque), and supplier tracking.

The operational cycle is straightforward: sample, analysis, weighing, and payment.

However, the buying point itself does not generate volume — it receives it.

Volume is created through a network of artisanal miners, small traders, and local contacts.

In practice, this involves advance payments, personal relationships, and consistent off-take.

In other words, flow goes where there is money, speed, and constant presence.

This is not a theoretical model — it is an operational environment.

At one point, I received about $5,000 in cash in Lokoja. The money was handed to someone I trusted to deposit into a bank account. He never returned. Later I heard he became a pastor.

In another case, before a trip to meet a processing partner, a partner attempted to replace me in the delegation with his own representative.

These are not exceptions. This is the environment.

The main risks at the buying stage are material quality, fraud, broken agreements, and direct financial losses.

This makes verification, control, and security essential.

Many operators rely on local intermediaries because they already have networks, operate within the system, and absorb part of the risk.

At the same time, direct sourcing provides better control over both flow and pricing.

Based on market conditions in 2025, at a volume of around 20 tons per month, purchase prices ranged between ₦2,800,000 and ₦3,000,000 per ton, with resale prices around ₦3,500,000 per ton.

This results in a margin of approximately ₦500,000 to ₦700,000 per ton.

Total operating expenses — including staff, rent, security, laboratory, logistics, and other costs — are approximately ₦80,000,000 per year.

The key factor is simple: the entire economics depend on the purchase price.

Even small changes at entry level significantly affect the outcome.

A buying point as a market entry provides what matters most: speed, network formation, volume control, and real market understanding.

In this model, everything is simple: if you do not control procurement, you do not control the business.

The alternative is a fully mechanized mining operation. It is a valid and profitable model, but it requires significantly higher capital and organizational capacity.

For that reason, it is typically considered as a next stage, alongside the development of processing capabilities, including ferroniobium production.
