Neo-Luddites of the 21st Century: Why the Fear of AI Is the Same Old Story
October 2025

Every technological leap creates two reactions at once: excitement and panic. The excitement tends to be quiet and focused — engineers, operators, founders. The panic is always loud and public. It demands a pause, a ban, a regulation, a moratorium. “We must stop this before it’s too late.”

We’ve seen this before. In the early 19th century, English textile workers smashed industrial looms. They weren’t just angry at machines; they were angry at the shift in power. The machine wasn’t only making production faster, it was changing who controlled production.

Today’s anti-AI rhetoric is the same song in a new arrangement. The request is framed as responsibility — “slow down AI for humanity” — but the subtext is the same as it was with the looms: “I am losing control over a system that used to depend on me.”

There is also a moral framing that repeats across centuries. The machine isn’t described as a tool. It’s described as a threat to order, dignity, identity. “It will replace us. It will erase what makes us human.” The irony is that every breakthrough in automation (from textile mechanization to ATMs to machine learning) initially sounds like the end of skilled work — and then, after the panic, it quietly becomes infrastructure.

Calls to “pause” AI often come from people and institutions who feel that the next phase will not be governed by their rules. The fear isn’t that AI will accelerate. The fear is that it will accelerate without them sitting in the control room.

Progress does not ask for permission. That line sounds cynical, but it’s actually descriptive. You can slow adoption in some jurisdictions. You can regulate. You can redirect who profits. You can criminalize certain uses. But you cannot uninvent capacity.

And once the capacity exists — to generate text, to generate images, to generate code, to generate strategy — it spreads. Whether or not someone in Washington, Brussels, or Davos is “comfortable.”

So yes, the fear around AI is understandable. It’s also familiar. We’ve seen it with industrial weaving machines, with electricity, with chemical fertilizers, with telecommunications, with cryptography, with the commercial internet.

“Stop the future” is always marketed as ethics. It’s usually about power.
