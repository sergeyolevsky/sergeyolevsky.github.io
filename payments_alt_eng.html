Alternative Payment Systems Under Sanctions: How They Actually Work
2026

After a number of Russian banks were disconnected from [SWIFT](chatgpt://generic-entity?number=0), alternative payment systems are often perceived as a technological replacement.

This is misleading. In practice, this is not a replacement but a shift to a different model of settlement — one with less transparency, more intermediaries, higher costs, and significantly higher risks.

When traditional infrastructure becomes unavailable, business does not stop. It adapts. The questions become practical: through whom can a payment be routed, in which jurisdiction can it be processed, and in which currency it can actually settle.

Alternative systems emerge at this exact point — not as innovation-driven products, but as a response to restricted access to infrastructure and liquidity.

Stripped of marketing language, the reality is straightforward. Payments rarely move directly. There is almost always a chain of participants. Each transaction becomes a balance between speed, cost, and risk.

Instead of a single operation, a sequence appears: splitting flows, changing currencies, routing through multiple jurisdictions. What emerges is not a “payment system” in the classical sense, but a coordination layer between actors who cannot execute cross-border payments on their own.

There is a common assumption that such systems operate fully outside the global financial architecture. In reality, this is not the case.

At some stage of the transaction chain, elements of traditional infrastructure inevitably appear — including systems like [SWIFT](chatgpt://generic-entity?number=1) or regional frameworks such as [SEPA](chatgpt://generic-entity?number=2).

This typically happens not at the point of initiation, but at the level of intermediaries. It is there that final settlement occurs and where funds enter the “regulated” financial environment.

Fully isolated payment systems do not exist in practice. The real question is at which point the connection to the global system becomes visible.

From a business perspective, the system works — funds eventually arrive. From a regulatory perspective, a different question arises: is the underlying transaction fully understood?

In many cases, the answer is no. This is where friction with regulators such as [OFAC](chatgpt://generic-entity?number=3) begins.

The issue is not the technology itself, but the architecture: lack of end-to-end transparency, difficulty in verifying beneficial ownership, fragmented routing across jurisdictions, and distributed control.

For the global financial system, this translates into loss of visibility. And loss of visibility is automatically treated as risk.

At the same time, such systems are rarely shut down immediately. While they operate, they reveal participants, routes, and structural dependencies. As a result, the typical sequence is observation, pressure on participants, and only then targeted restrictions.

What emerges is a parallel layer of infrastructure — more expensive, less stable, but functionally necessary. It exists alongside the global system, but does not replace it.

Alternative payment systems are therefore less about circumvention as an idea, and more about adaptation. However, this adaptation remains structurally dependent on the very global infrastructure it attempts to bypass.
