Solutions Are Not Always Where You Look
April 2026

I have long come to a simple conclusion: many problems are not solved through direct, indirect, or “roundabout” approaches.

They are solved adjacently.

On one condition — you understand what you are doing.

Here is an example. It is no longer sensitive, so it can be discussed openly.

At one point, a large amount of Russian funds accumulated in India in local currency — proceeds from oil sales.

We are talking about billions of dollars in equivalent.

These funds could not be freely converted.
They could not be easily repatriated.
They were effectively “stuck”.

What followed was predictable.

Everything was used at once.

Banks.
Government-level arrangements.
Crypto.
Mirror schemes.
Various structures.

Given the scale, one can imagine how much time, money, and effort went into trying to solve the problem directly.

But there was another approach.

Not direct.
Not workaround.
Adjacent.

At that time, a payment company in Dubai was for sale — let us call it GCC Remit.

In essence, a classic remittance system.

A model familiar to everyone through Western Union:

cash-in without opening an account
transfer
cash-out in another country through an agent network

GCC had infrastructure:

payout locations
partner banks
local payment companies

And a key element — prefunding.

Advance funding.

Simply put: to pay out in India, money must already be there.

Now an important point.

The corridor “Gulf region → India” is one of the largest in the world.

Tens of billions of dollars annually.

Migrant workers send money home.
The system must deliver payouts.

Which means — liquidity must constantly be available in India.

And this is where the solution appears.

If you have:

your own sending infrastructure (like GCC)
and funds stuck in India

you do not need to extract them.

You use them where they already are.

You do not change the money.

You change its function.

The rupees stop being “trapped funds”.

They become:

funding
liquidity
fuel for the remittance system

From there, the logic is straightforward.

You accept transfers in Dubai and across the region.

You pay out in India using existing liquidity.

On the sending side, you generate revenue —
in freely convertible currencies such as USD and AED.

As a result:

the funds remain in India,
but they start working.

And not just working —
generating hard currency income.

This is not an instant solution.
It requires infrastructure, control, and understanding.

But it is no longer about extracting funds.

It is about building a system that earns on them.

And most importantly — it scales.

The same logic applies:

in Africa
in the Middle East
in any region with capital controls and active money flows

If you have:

network
access
understanding of mechanics

Most people look for solutions within the problem.

But often the solution sits next to it — in an adjacent system.

Some try to recover money.

Others start earning on it.
