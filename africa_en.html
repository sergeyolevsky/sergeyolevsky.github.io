Working in Africa
October 2025

Africa is often described as a continent of opportunities — and it truly is. But it is also a continent of deep-rooted traditions, layered power systems, and complex inter-ethnic dynamics. Without understanding those realities, no project — no matter how well-funded or technically sound — will last.

Take Nigeria as an example. With over 140 major ethnic groups and three dominant centers of gravity — Hausa in the north, Yoruba in the west, and Igbo in the east — political balance is never just institutional. It’s historical and tribal.

Modern governance in Nigeria coexists with a system of traditional rulers — kings, emirs, obas — who hold influence that no constitution can replace. They are the link between local legitimacy and federal power. Even the most centralized government depends on their consent.

This is the first rule of working in Africa: formal authority is not the same as real authority. A governor may have an office; a traditional chief has loyalty. And in many cases, loyalty is what matters.

Western companies often underestimate this. They arrive with contracts and business logic, expecting a rule-based environment — only to discover that trust, reputation, and informal consensus carry more weight than written agreements. This is not corruption. It is continuity.

Successful partnerships in Africa are built not just through capital, but through presence. Being there, meeting elders, understanding cultural codes — that’s how legitimacy is earned. Once you are seen as a partner, not a visitor, the landscape opens up.

In that sense, working in Africa is not “different.” It’s simply more human. The relationships come first; the business follows.
