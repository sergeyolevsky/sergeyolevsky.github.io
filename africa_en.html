Working in Africa: Lessons from Nigeria
October 2025

When I first came to Nigeria, I thought I had done my homework. I had read the reports, learned the structure of the federal system, and studied the main industries. But very soon, I realized that the country I saw on paper and the Nigeria I saw on the ground were two different realities.

Nigeria is not a single market. It is a mosaic of over a hundred ethnic groups, languages, faiths, and traditional hierarchies. Every project, every negotiation, passes through invisible layers of influence. Federal laws and official agreements matter, of course ‚Äî but they are only the surface. Beneath them lies the living structure of traditional power: chiefs, emirs, and community leaders whose authority is not symbolic but functional.

Many international companies learn this lesson the hard way. Shell, Texaco, and Mobil succeeded in Nigeria not because they had more money or better lawyers, but because they understood the rules of engagement: they built relationships, respected local authority, and invested in communities before expecting production.

Our company, Mining Technologies Co. Ltd (Jos, Nigeria), built the first fully mechanized columbite (Nb-Ta) mine in modern, post-colonial Nigeria.
