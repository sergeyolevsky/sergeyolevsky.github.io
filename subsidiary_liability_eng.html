A Story That Lasted More Than Eight Years — Two of Them Under Subsidiary Liability
March 2026

It seems that my long story with subsidiary liability in a bankruptcy case involving one of the companies operating under the ChronoPay brand has finally come to an end.

The process lasted more than eight years. For the last two of those years I personally remained under subsidiary liability. I will not describe how unpleasant that experience can be — anyone who has gone through something similar already understands it.

Of course, the situation was influenced by the negative information background surrounding the entire ChronoPay story — not only because of earlier events, but also in connection with a new criminal case involving the main shareholder of ChronoPay.

I joined ChronoPay in 2014. As I later learned, the company’s shareholder had only recently been released following the well-known case involving Assist and Aeroflot.

The invitation to work there happened quite unexpectedly — he wrote to me on LinkedIn.

At that time I had just completed my second period of work at Unistream Bank and was looking for a new project. Shortly before that I had an online interview with the head of HR at Yandex.Money. The impression it left was so negative that I openly wrote about it on LinkedIn. Those posts, as it later turned out, caught the attention of Pavel Vrublevsky.

After our conversation he offered me the position of head of the department responsible for relations with banks and payment systems.

Toward the end of the conversation — apparently realizing that I might not fully appreciate his already well-known reputation in the online payments industry — he asked whether I was concerned that he had just been released after a high-profile cybercrime case.

My answer was quite simple.

I grew up in Moscow in the 1980s and 1990s in Maryina Roshcha, so I said roughly this: if we have decided to work together, then we will work together. Life can be complicated, and things are not always fair.

Events moved rather quickly after that.

First I became head of the department responsible for relations with banks and payment systems. Later I became executive director, and about six months before leaving the company I was appointed general director.

There were objective reasons for these changes. At that time we were discussing an ambitious idea — to build a full-fledged payment system on the basis of ChronoPay. We were already in discussions with several banks, some of which looked like very promising partners.

I worked at ChronoPay until mid-2015. After that I left the company and spent some time in San Francisco.

About a year and a half after my departure, an attempt was made by a new management team to initiate bankruptcy proceedings against one of the companies associated with the ChronoPay brand.

A long court process began.

For quite a long time I essentially ignored it, for several reasons. The main one was simple: I clearly understood that I had nothing to do with the decisions and processes that took place after my departure from the company. During my time there everything had been conducted properly.

Nevertheless, sometime in 2023 an enforcement order unexpectedly reached me, which caused a fair amount of trouble.

Over the past months I began regularly attending the court hearings and, frankly, I regretted not doing so earlier.

But most importantly, during this time I became convinced of one simple thing: the system does work.

For that reason I would like to thank everyone who helped me navigate the process and the hearings themselves.

Fiat justitia, ruat caelum.
