Tokenisation of Settlements: The Revolution Almost Nobody Noticed
April 2026

While everyone is discussing SWIFT, crypto and central bank digital currencies, something more interesting is quietly happening.

The tokenisation of assets and settlements is not the next wave. It is already here. Most market participants either call it by different names or simply do not notice it until they encounter it in practice.

Let me explain with a simple example.

Imagine a trading company. It has goods in a warehouse in Dubai. The buyer is in India. The financing comes from Europe.

Under the old model, the settlement goes through a chain of banks: a correspondent in London, a correspondent in Singapore, and then a local bank.

At every stage, there is a check. At every stage, there is a delay. At every stage, there is a fee. And at every stage, there is a risk that the payment will be returned without a clear explanation.

Now imagine the same transaction with a tokenised asset.

The goods are represented by a digital token — not a cryptocurrency, but a digital representation of ownership or a claim. Financing can be provided against that token. The buyer receives the rights once the payment conditions are met — almost instantly and without a long chain of correspondent banks.

The structure becomes simpler. Speed increases. Cost decreases. Counterparty risk is reduced.

This is not theoretical. Major banks are already working on it in trade finance, interbank settlement and treasury operations.

But the key point is this.

Tokenisation is not about blockchain itself. Blockchain is just infrastructure. In the same way, SWIFT is not about payments, but about messaging between banks.

What actually changes is the moment of settlement.

In the traditional model, ownership and money move separately and at different times. That is where delays, risks and intermediaries come from.

Tokenisation allows synchronisation of asset transfer and payment. This is delivery versus payment — DVP. The principle is old, but it is now moving into trade finance and cross-border settlements.

When transfer and payment happen simultaneously, direct counterparty risk is reduced.

Trust does not disappear. It shifts into infrastructure — platforms, custodians, legal frameworks and settlement assets.

Where does this already work?

Trade finance — yes. Banks are already digitising and tokenising instruments.

Interbank settlement — partially. Real transactions have been executed, but not at scale yet.

Corporate treasury — increasingly. Tokenised liquidity instruments are emerging.

Retail payments — not yet. Regulation and compliance remain major barriers.

Emerging markets — uneven. Interest is high, but infrastructure is still developing.

What does this mean in practice?

The next few years will be a period of parallel systems.

The winners will not be those who adopt first, but those who can operate across both systems.

Regulatory clarity will lag behind — as always.

One final point.

“Tokenisation” is becoming a marketing word.

But behind the noise, something real is changing.

The mechanics of settlement are shifting.

And once that happens, the rest follows.
