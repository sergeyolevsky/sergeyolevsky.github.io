Tokenisation of Settlements: The Revolution Almost Nobody Noticed
April 2026

While everyone is discussing SWIFT, crypto and central bank digital currencies, something more interesting is quietly happening.

The tokenisation of assets and settlements is not the next wave. It is already underway. Most market participants either call it by different names or simply do not notice it until they encounter it in practice.

Let me explain with a specific example.

Imagine a trading company. It has goods stored in a warehouse in Dubai. The buyer is in India. The financing comes from Europe.

Under the old model, the settlement goes through a chain of banks: a correspondent bank in London, a correspondent bank in Singapore, and then a local bank.

At every link in the chain, there is a check. At every link, there is a delay. At every link, there is a fee. And at every link, there is a risk that the payment will be returned without explanation.

Now take the same transaction — but with a tokenised asset.

The goods in the warehouse are represented by a digital token on a blockchain platform — not a cryptocurrency, but a digital confirmation of ownership rights or a claim. Financing can be provided against that token as collateral. The buyer receives the right to the goods once the payment conditions are fulfilled — almost instantly and without a long chain of correspondent banks.

The structure becomes simpler. Speed increases. Cost decreases. Counterparty risk is reduced.

This is not science fiction. Major banks such as JPMorgan, HSBC and Standard Chartered are already testing this in trade finance, interbank settlements and corporate treasury operations.

But here is what matters from a practical point of view.

Tokenisation is not about blockchain itself. Blockchain here is only infrastructure. In the same way, SWIFT is not about payments themselves, but about messaging between banks.

The essence is elsewhere.

Tokenisation changes the moment of settlement.

In the traditional model, ownership rights and money move on different rails — and at different times. This is where so many problems in international settlements come from: delays, risks and the need for intermediaries.

A tokenised settlement makes it possible to synchronise the transfer of the asset and the payment. This is called delivery versus payment — DVP. The principle is old and comes from securities markets. But now it is gradually moving into trade finance, corporate settlements and new forms of cross-border payments.

When the transfer of the asset and the payment happen at the same time, the need to rely directly on the counterparty is reduced.

Trust does not disappear completely. It moves into the infrastructure: the platform, the custodian, the legal structure, the smart contract and the settlement asset. But the risk of non-performance becomes lower.

Now let us look at where this already works — and where it does not yet.

It works in trade finance. Major banks are using digital and tokenised instruments for letters of credit, guarantees and document management. The time needed to issue and verify such instruments is being reduced from weeks to hours.

It works in interbank settlements. Central banks and commercial banks in Singapore, the UAE, Hong Kong and other jurisdictions have already conducted real cross-border transactions using central bank digital currencies and shared platforms. This is not yet mass infrastructure, but it is no longer pure theory either.

It works in corporate treasury operations. Companies are beginning to hold part of their liquidity in tokenised money market funds and other digital instruments that are available almost around the clock and can be converted more quickly.

It does not yet work in mass retail remittances. Regulation, identification, consumer protection and operational risks still create barriers that have not been overcome.

It does not yet work at full scale in emerging markets. Not because there is no interest. The problem is infrastructure: reliable custodians, legal frameworks, liquidity in local assets and clear rules for banks. Still, Nigeria, Kenya and India are moving faster than many expected.

What follows from this for those working in international payments?

First. The next three to five years will be a period of parallel systems. The old infrastructure will not disappear: too much capital is locked into it, and too many processes depend on it. But a new infrastructure is already operating alongside it — and the efficiency gap will continue to grow.

Second. The competitive advantage will not belong simply to those who adopt tokenisation first. It will belong to those who can operate in both worlds at the same time — moving transactions between old and new rails depending on the corridor, amount, currency, jurisdiction and counterparty.

Third. Regulatory uncertainty will remain. But this is not a reason to wait for perfect clarity. Those who wait for ideal certainty usually arrive late. In the payments industry, operating under incomplete clarity has always been part of the job — provided the risks and limitations are understood.

And one final point.

I see how the word “tokenisation” is turning into a marketing label — just as “blockchain” and “fintech” once did. It is being attached to almost everything.

That should not be a reason to dismiss it. Beneath the noise, there is real substance. The mechanics of settlement are changing. Intermediaries that existed only because “this is how it has always been done” will gradually be washed out. Not tomorrow — but no longer twenty years from now either.

The payments industry has always developed in the same way: long periods of inertia, followed by rapid shifts that seem sudden only to those who were not watching.

This is one of those shifts.
