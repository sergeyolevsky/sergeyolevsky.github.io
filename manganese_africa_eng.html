Manganese. Africa: The Klondike Moscow Never Saw
June 2026

I have always kept in mind that Africa is a kind of Klondike that many in Moscow simply refused to notice, except for one man with a heavy past whose plane was later blown up.

My late father — and largely because of his fatal mistake in 1994 — is how I first became connected with Nigeria in 1997. He always said about Nigeria: the natural wealth is unbelievable, everything is literally under your feet — gold, tantalum, zinc — as if you just stick a rod into the ground and it grows.

In 1994 he was a relatively large businessman from the early cooperative era, dealing with typical problems of the time — competitors, former partners, pressure from banks and creditors. He needed to urgently close financial gaps.

Through an acquaintance — an officer — whose son promised “fast and profitable money” through Nigerian contacts, he got involved in a quick-money scheme in Nigeria. In the end, 680,000 dollars were simply stolen by Nigerian, at that time still relatively unknown fraudsters.

This could easily be a separate book. For now, I will just say that my father and the officer’s son traveled to Nigeria in June 1994 for just two weeks in an attempt to recover the money.

The money was not returned, but I still remember the name of one of the people involved — Francis Shanu. He was eventually imprisoned.

From that point on — without drama — I have been connected to this region for almost 30 years.

One of the cases went like this: colleagues and partners asked me to find manganese in Africa. I started analyzing the market, went through South Africa via university contacts and friends, but quickly discovered that all available volumes had already been sold under offtake agreements for a year ahead.

So I turned to Nigerian contacts to help identify alternatives through their connections in South Africa. And at that moment it became clear that manganese also exists in Nigeria.

Formally, it barely appears in export statistics, but in practice it exists, and a significant part of the flow has long been moving toward China.

China’s growing footprint in Africa was already obvious in the early 2000s. Today, however, it is no longer just trade presence.

In Nigeria, it has effectively become a parallel economic structure within the state.

Chinese companies are present in oil, banking, infrastructure, mining, railways, and airports.

When I discussed manganese with Nigerians, I asked a simple question: how did China end up taking everything? The answer was simple — just like lithium: first volumes were quietly secured, then it became too late to change anything.

Africa today is not just a market or a resource base. It is a space where stable external economic influence structures have already been formed.

Meanwhile, Moscow still lacks a coherent understanding of how to enter Nigeria in a systematic way. At the same time, within Nigeria, there is a growing fatigue with Chinese dominance — and a demand for new players, including in manganese, lithium, and other sectors.

Russia has been working with Nigeria since the 1990s, but still lacks stable entry models, long-term structures, or meaningful consolidation.
