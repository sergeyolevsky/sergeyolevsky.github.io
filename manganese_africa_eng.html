Manganese. Africa: The Klondike Many in Moscow Never Saw
June 2026

I have always had a strong sense that Africa is a kind of modern Klondike — a region of opportunities that many in Moscow simply refused to notice.

My late father — and it was partly because of a costly mistake in the mid-1990s that I first became closely connected to Nigeria — used to talk about the country in very simple terms: extraordinary natural wealth, ideal climate conditions, and resources literally lying under your feet. Gold, tantalum, zinc — sometimes it feels as if you could just plant a shovel in the ground and it would grow.

In 1994, he was a fairly prominent businessman from the early cooperative era. Like many entrepreneurs of that time, he faced pressure from competitors and former partners, including indirect pressure on creditor banks. He urgently needed liquidity to close certain financial gaps.

Through a connection — an officer whose son was allegedly involved in “very interesting and profitable” projects with Nigerians — he entered into a deal that looked convincing at the time. As a result, what were then very significant funds — around 680,000 USD — were effectively stolen by Nigerian fraudsters who were still relatively unknown at that time.

My father went to Nigeria in June 1994 together with the son of that acquaintance, planning to recover the funds within two weeks.

He ended up staying for almost nine years. The money was never returned, but one of the individuals connected to the case — Francis Shanu — was eventually arrested. I still remember the name.

I personally arrived in Nigeria in 1997. Since then, my connection with the region has lasted for almost three decades.

Recently, colleagues asked me to help identify manganese sources in Africa. I started by looking at South Africa, assuming it would be the natural starting point. It turned out that while manganese exists there, most production is already tied up in off-take agreements for the next year or more.

When I turned to Nigeria through local contacts, I discovered something interesting: manganese exists there as well. Officially, Nigeria is not a major exporter, but in practice the material is present and largely absorbed by Chinese buyers.

It has been clear since the early 2000s that China is building a parallel economic structure across parts of Africa. Today this presence spans oil, banking, infrastructure, railways, airports, and natural resources — and in some places even extends into informal security ecosystems.

When I discussed manganese with Nigerian partners, they explained that this pattern is not new. The same thing happened earlier with lithium: assets were quietly consolidated over time until the market structure became almost irreversible.

The key question is not whether resources exist. It is whether anyone is actually positioned to access them.

Structurally, Africa is not just a “market”. It is a layered system of states, informal economies, and competing external actors. Understanding that structure is far more important than any single commodity.

There is still a window of opportunity to build serious resource positions — but it requires presence, infrastructure, and the ability to operate in complex local environments rather than viewing the region from a distance.
