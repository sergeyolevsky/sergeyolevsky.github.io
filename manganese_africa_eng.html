Manganese. Africa: The Klondike Many in Moscow Failed to See
June 2026

I have always had in mind that Africa is a kind of Klondike that many Russian representatives simply refused to see, except perhaps one man with a heavy past whose plane later exploded.

My late father, and specifically a fatal mistake he made in 1994, is what first led me to Nigeria in 1997. He used to say: Nigeria is a place of extraordinary natural wealth and climate conditions — everything is under your feet: gold, tantalum, zinc. You can literally stick a shovel into the ground and something will grow.

In 1994 he was a fairly significant businessman from the first wave of Soviet cooperatives, facing typical problems of that era — pressure from competitors and former partners, influence over creditor banks, and urgent financial gaps that needed to be closed.

Through an acquaintance — an officer — whose son claimed to be involved in “very interesting and profitable projects” with Nigerians, he was drawn into a fast-money scheme in Nigeria. In the end, 680,000 dollars simply disappeared, stolen by Nigerian scammers who at that time were still relatively unknown.

It could easily be the subject of a full book. I will limit myself to the fact that in June 1994 my father and this officer’s son went to Nigeria for two weeks to try to recover the money.

The money was never returned, but one of those involved — a man I still remember by name, Francis Shanu — was eventually arrested.

Leaving the personal story aside, I have been connected to this region for almost 30 years.

At one point, colleagues asked me to find manganese in Africa. I studied the market, spoke to contacts, classmates and friends, and initially looked at South Africa. It turned out manganese does exist there, but practically all supply is already locked under long-term offtake agreements.

That is when I turned to Nigerian contacts to help me understand whether South Africa could be accessed through regional networks. Instead, I discovered something else: manganese also exists in Nigeria.

Formally, it barely appears in export statistics, but in practice it exists, and most of the flow is already absorbed by Chinese buyers.

China’s growing presence in Africa was already obvious in the early 2000s. Today, in countries like Nigeria, it is no longer just commercial presence — it has become a parallel economic structure operating inside the state itself.

Chinese companies are present across oil, banking, infrastructure, mining, railways and airports. Even in the informal economy, there are cases of direct competition between Chinese and local structures.

When discussing manganese with Nigerian partners, I asked how it happened that Chinese actors control so much of the flow. The answer was simple: it is similar to lithium — early, quiet accumulation of control until the market becomes effectively closed.

In my view, there is still a possibility to build a serious resource base there. Bring in your own security structure, build operations properly, and many Nigerians would actually prefer an alternative to Chinese dominance.

But there is still no clear understanding in Moscow of how this environment actually works. Nigeria has been discussed since the 1990s, yet very little structured has been built on the ground.
