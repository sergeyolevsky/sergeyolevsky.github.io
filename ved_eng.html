International Trade Does Not Start with Logistics — It Starts with One Question: What Exactly Are You Shipping?
April 2026

Recently I had a conversation about international trade.

Not about cross-border payments or international settlements — that is closer to my core area of work. This was about classical foreign trade. The old-fashioned kind, where Incoterms, logistics, customs, trade finance and all the things many people somehow consider secondary suddenly become the real substance of the matter.

And, as usual, I asked a simple question:

— Who is your client? What exactly are you shipping?

They looked at me with mild surprise.

— Why does that matter?

This is usually where the interesting part begins.

What do you mean — why?

What are we even talking about?

Because raw materials are one story.

There you immediately have FOB, CIF, pre-advice, letters of credit, inspection companies such as SGS or Alfred H. Knight, laboratory analysis, quality issues, bank guarantees and disputes over specifications.

Consumer goods are a completely different logic.

Electronics are a third one.

Equipment is a fourth.

Dual-use goods are already a separate world, where many people suddenly start speaking more quietly and choosing their words more carefully.

Transport?

Sea freight has one economy and one set of risks.

Air freight is a completely different story.

Road transport is a third one.

And somewhere nearby, customs value adjustment starts to appear on the horizon.

For those who have not dealt with it: customs value adjustment is when customs decides that you bought the goods “too cheaply” and offers you its own version of economic reality.

And customs can sometimes have a very creative view of reality.

Then they tell me:

— We move something from China. We would prefer not to attract unnecessary visibility in the United States, and here we want everything to be quiet, calm, without extra noise — but with good terms.

— What does that mean? — I ask.

— Well, we want the total cost related to payments, delivery and customs to be better than others have. Maybe we could even pay in crypto.

And at moments like this, I usually understand that people are not looking for a solution.

They are looking for magic.

Or, to put it more honestly, for a universal button that would make everything at once:

cheap,

fast,

quiet,

safe,

legal,

and preferably independent from everyone else.

It does not work like that.

Or almost never.

— Are you trying to smuggle something? — I ask.

They look surprised.

— No, of course not.

— Then this is definitely not for me, — I say. — I have never worked in customs, and stories about how to live outside the system are not my field. My task is usually different: to understand how to build a working and sustainable model inside the reality that actually exists.

— It is just that the client is difficult.

— What exactly are you shipping?

Silence.

— Electronics? Household appliances? Or something dual-use?

Silence again.

— Can no one help you with customs?

— They can. But we do not want to depend on anyone. We want to do it ourselves.

And this is where I usually understand that the conversation is only beginning.

Because most people treat international trade as a collection of separate services.

Payment separately.

Delivery separately.

Customs separately.

Documents separately.

Bank separately.

Freight forwarder separately.

Someone who “can solve things” separately.

In practice, it does not work like that.

International trade is a system.

And it is a system of compromises.

Between price and speed.

Between transparency and flexibility.

Between compliance and operational resilience.

Between the desire “not to attract attention” and the objective reality in which the goods still pass through banks, carriers, insurers, customs, certification bodies, inspection companies and dozens of people you will never see.

Especially in Russia.

I live here, so I have had no illusions for a long time.

In Russian foreign trade practice, formal process and informal practical experience often move side by side.

Whether one likes it or not.

Not because everyone wants to break the rules.

And not because everyone dreams of “bypassing” something.

But because between a textbook on international trade and real life there is a huge gap made of sanctions, bank compliance, customs, logistical restrictions, jurisdictions, human factors and sometimes simple absurdity.

Still, there is one mistake I see again and again.

People start with the wrong question.

They ask:

— How do we ship it?

But the right question is almost always different:

— What exactly are you shipping, to whom and why?

Because the product itself determines almost everything else.

The route.

The bank.

The delivery terms.

The currency.

The insurance.

The settlement logic.

The probability of problems at customs.

The compliance risk.

Even how nervous the people in the chain will become.

I said one thing to them then, and I will repeat it here.

Every customs checkpoint is like a separate country.

With its own anthem.

Its own central bank.

And its own constitution.

Those who have worked in international trade will understand immediately.

The rest will find out eventually.
