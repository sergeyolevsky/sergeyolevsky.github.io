Payment Systems
May 2025

When people talk about “cutting a country off from SWIFT,” it usually sounds like a financial death sentence. In reality, the situation is more technical — and less dramatic.

SWIFT is not a payment system in the strict sense. It does not move money. It does not settle transactions. What SWIFT actually does is deliver structured messages between banks and other financial institutions. Those messages tell banks: who is paying whom, through which intermediaries, in what currency, under which reference.

In other words, SWIFT is infrastructure for financial communication, not financial execution. Money still moves through correspondent banking, nostro/vostro accounts, central bank links, local clearing rails, and so on — not “through SWIFT.” SWIFT is the standardized language and the courier, not the wire itself.

This is why “disconnection from SWIFT” is often misunderstood. It is painful, but it is not the same thing as “a country can no longer move money across borders.” What it really means is: you lose access to the dominant global messaging network that most counterparties are comfortable with. You’re forced to fall back to less convenient channels.

Are there alternatives to SWIFT? Yes. Regional messaging systems, bank-to-bank bilateral arrangements, manual confirmation flows, custom APIs, payment aggregators that sit between regions. None of this is elegant. All of it increases friction, cost, and compliance risk. But in practice, cross-border value can still move.

So the point is not “no money moves without SWIFT.” The point is leverage. SWIFT is powerful because the network trusts it, not because it is the only technical way to send money. Cutting access is pressure, not a literal shutdown of the pipes.

The paradox is this: the more politicized access to SWIFT becomes, the faster alternative messaging channels develop. They may be clumsy and expensive at first — but they mature. And once they mature, global financial messaging is no longer a monopoly.
