How Tantalum Suddenly Turned Into Uranium
May 2026

As a follow-up to my article on international trade, I remembered one rather revealing episode.

As often happens, it all started in Nigeria.

And it ended with conversations about uranium, international banks, the Islamic world, local intelligence services, and the realization that sometimes the best business is the one you do not do.

International trade tends to produce stories like this.

People usually imagine foreign trade in a fairly simple way.

Contract.

Logistics.

Letter of credit.

Customs.

Loaded.

Shipped.

Paid.

In practice, everything sometimes begins to live a life of its own.

Especially when raw materials are involved.

And especially when it is Nigeria.

This was sometime in the mid-1990s.

We were sitting at the house of a Nigerian Air Marshal — Ibrahim Alfa.

His house was large, alive, always full of people. The Marshal himself was an interesting man — very educated, with a broad international outlook. He had studied and lived in the United States and Europe, had visited Moscow back in Soviet times, and had even been to Baikonur.

He was, one might say, not exactly an ordinary person for the Nigerian environment.

At some point, members of the household began to appear.

He pointed and said:

— This is Aisha, my wife.

Then he pointed to another woman:

— And this is my senior wife.

I no longer remember her name — we hardly crossed paths. But Aisha, who was not yet thirty at the time, often joined us at meetings, parties and various events.

Nigeria very quickly teaches a person that the structure of the world can be far more complicated than what he was told at home.

At that time, the Marshal headed one of our holdings and was a man who could open doors at levels where no one simply walks in.

When we needed to find a way to reach the leadership of Kazatomprom, it was he who wrote a letter to Mukhtar Dzhakishev.

And — importantly — Dzhakishev replied.

That, in essence, is how it all began.

After that, people from Kazatomprom and Ust-Kamenogorsk began coming to us — from the Ulba Metallurgical Plant.

At that time, the director there was the late Valery Timofeyevich Zhukov.

The surnames of their directors seemed almost deliberately selected.

After Zhukov came a man named Gagarin.

Zhukov arrived then with two ladies.

Quite theatrical businesswomen.

One was from Almaty.

The other lived permanently in Switzerland.

At the Ulba Metallurgical Plant, we were primarily interested in the tantalum plant.

Although, of course, there was much more there — uranium production, related facilities, and issues connected with the storage of radioactive materials.

At that time, we were mining columbite.

Tantalite — we were buying.

And here there is an important technical point.

In our columbite concentrate from the Jos area, tantalum was usually present in relatively small quantities — the content of tantalum pentoxide (Ta₂O₅) was often around 6–8%. The main metal was niobium.

In tantalite, it is the other way around: Ta₂O₅ is significantly higher, while niobium is much lower.

And that material already cost very different money.

The problem was working capital.

Tantalite was expensive.

It required free cash, and quite a lot of it.

Zhukov partly helped us with this issue.

Ulba would issue letters of credit, against which we could obtain credit financing.

For Nigeria, to put it mildly, this was not unimportant.

And then, during one of the discussions, a topic came up almost by accident — a topic that until then had been somewhere on the periphery.

Uranium.

And thorium.

They also occur in tantalite concentrate.

Usually — in very small quantities.

If the indicators were within permissible limits, the logistics worked fairly straightforwardly.

The concentrate was accepted for shipment, went through Lagos, then by sea — usually to Rotterdam, where assays were carried out again, the goods were checked against the contractual parameters, and only after that the letter of credit was released.

If the quality matched the contract and the bill of lading data, everything worked like clockwork.

If not, problems began.

And the main indicator here was not only the Ta₂O₅ content.

Harmful impurities were also monitored.

Above all — uranium and thorium.

If the indicators exceeded the permissible level, the goods literally disappeared.

That is why Nigerian tantalite with elevated uranium and thorium content was often noticeably cheaper.

It could only be used in small proportions, blended with cleaner concentrate, if the average indicators of the shipment allowed it.

And then, at some point, Zhukov suddenly said:

— A pity. At Ulba, I would separate the uranium and thorium and dispose of them.

We looked at him:

— Yes, but how would we even deliver it?

And then the Air Marshal unexpectedly joined in:

— Then you have to fly.

And added:

— Without any stops in places like Rotterdam.

Zhukov thought for a moment.

Then he said:

— We will figure it out.

— Men, this has to be done.

— This is our chance.

And to be honest, the economics then really looked almost unbelievable.

So much so that even today, years later, I sometimes ask myself: could it really have been that profitable?

But it could.

If the raw material was purchased at a price close to columbite — because for less money the artisanal miners simply would not wash the ore — and then processed into finished metallic tantalum, the economics looked almost fantastic.

At least on paper.

And sometimes it is precisely this combination that is most dangerous.

When everything looks too good.

When it seems that the chance has finally appeared.

When logic begins pushing a person toward the thought:

if we do not do it, someone else will.

But in Nigeria there is one rule that anyone who works there long enough understands fairly quickly.

On sensitive subjects, you cannot work in the dark with local partners.

They may take offense.

And after that — who knows.

Sometimes the consequences are financial.

Sometimes — not only financial.

So we decided to do everything as openly as possible within our circle.

At the time, we were working with Diamond Bank and its head — Pascal Dozie. I have spoken about him before.

Pascal was a very intelligent and cautious man, and he understood some things much better than we did — things that at that moment seemed to us to be just business.

We came to him.

Explained the whole structure.

Calculated the economics.

We spoke almost with enthusiasm.

If everything went as planned, we said, we would make enough money to take control of Ajaokuta and complete it.

For those who do not know: Ajaokuta is a huge metallurgical project in Kogi State.

The Soviet Union built it as a future industrial center for Nigeria.

Then everything stopped.

The plant never fully started operating.

This was sometime in the mid-1990s.

And, symbolically, even today the story of Ajaokuta is still not fully over.

The conversation then was almost euphoric.

We were already beginning to see ahead of us not just a good business, but something much larger.

Pascal listened to us very carefully.

He did not say anything immediately.

He only said:

— Give me time to think.

A couple of weeks later, he came back with an answer.

Short.

Calm.

And very firm.

Diamond Bank would not be involved in this.

Not directly.

Not indirectly.

Not in any form.

Anything even remotely close to uranium was of no interest.

At the time, we explained this to ourselves rather simply.

Pascal worked very closely with British and American financial structures.

And their attitude toward such things was always, let us say, especially sensitive.

Especially when Nigeria, raw materials, complex logistics and even the slightest mention of uranium appeared somewhere on the horizon.

But the idea still would not let us go.

It all looked too beautiful.

So we went to another person.

I will not name him.

He remains a significant figure to this day — not only in Africa.

Let me put it this way: he was a man with old, very deep and serious connections inside the Islamic world — business, family and political connections.

So deep that some doors simply opened for him by themselves.

We told him the whole story.

He listened calmly.

Thought for a moment.

And said:

— There is a bank.

I will not name it either.

— We will meet.

We met the CEO of the bank.

And there the conversation went very differently.

He said:

— I do not see a problem.

— One million.

Two.

Three.

As much as needed?

— You will buy the concentrate yourselves.

I will finance the logistics and processing in Kazakhstan.

And what was most interesting — in reality, not that much money was needed.

But we did not have that kind of free cash anyway.

We found money for the concentrate.

We began carefully spreading word in the market that we were prepared to buy exactly this kind of tantalite.

And literally about a month later — when we had only just begun trial purchases — a message came.

Unofficial.

Through acquaintances.

From people connected to local intelligence services.

Very short.

Do not even think about doing this.

And that was the moment when we understood rather quickly that sometimes it is better to understand a hint correctly.

Especially in Nigeria.

Especially when the story begins to intersect with uranium.

And especially when international money, sensitive logistics and structures deeply integrated into the Islamic business world appear nearby.

We then quite quickly made our conclusion:

this was not something to pursue.

Even if the economics looked fantastic.

Even if it seemed like a chance.

Even if the money was almost lying on the table.

Because one of the things you learn over time in international trade, and in international business more broadly, is very simple.

Not every deal that can be done should be done.

Sometimes the best contract is the one you never signed.
