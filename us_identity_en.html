Political Culture: The United States on the Threshold of Two Identities
November 2025

American political institutions are commonly perceived as stable and self-regulating: separation of powers, federalism, state autonomy, a developed electoral system, and judicial oversight. Yet the resilience of a state depends not only on its formal mechanisms but also on a shared symbolic space in which those mechanisms are seen as legitimate.

For two centuries, American political culture rested on the idea of a common project ‚Äî the belief that the state provides the framework for collective action among groups with different interests and worldviews. As long as that framework held, conflicts remained internal and manageable. Today, that framework is under strain.

The State in Conservative Discourse

Since the mid-20th century, conservative rhetoric has increasingly framed the state as an external entity capable of restricting personal and economic freedom. This does not imply a rejection of institutions but weakens the perception of the state as a shared resource. If the state is seen as something ‚Äúother,‚Äù the outcomes of its procedures ‚Äî including elections ‚Äî are more readily perceived as imposed rather than collectively produced. This reduces political legitimacy.

Shift in Liberal Cultural Politics

At the same time, liberal discourse has foregrounded identity politics. While aimed at recognizing diversity, it has led to symbolic exclusion: certain groups are not removed from political procedures but from cultural acknowledgment as equal participants in the shared civic project.

Breakdown of the Shared Identity Framework

These trajectories reinforce one another: one part of society views the state as a controlling apparatus; another sees parts of society as culturally ‚Äúincomplete.‚Äù Formally, the system remains unified, but the symbolic foundation of a shared identity no longer is.

A New Definition of ‚ÄúWe‚Äù

The United States is not collapsing but undergoing a reconfiguration of its political culture. The country remains institutionally unified but divided in its understanding of what constitutes the shared project. The future depends on whether a new language of collective identity can be articulated ‚Äî or whether fragmentation becomes the stable norm.
