What You Need to Understand Before Going to Mine Gold in Africa
June 2026

I can speak primarily about alluvial gold, because the technology of mining and processing this type of gold is something I understand well.

Everything begins with geology. You must either conduct it or, at the very least, verify it. In practice, verification is still geology — simply in a more simplified form: pits, sampling, laboratory analysis. There is no way around this, even if you can already see illegal artisanal miners working the area and washing gold. A proper geological assessment usually takes several months.

To mine successfully, you need to understand the structure of the ore body, its location, thickness, metal concentration, and the nature of the deposit. All of this will ultimately determine your mining lease, the technology you use, the equipment you purchase, and the overall economics of the project.

The logic is straightforward: initial assessment, mining lease, geology — and only then extraction.

If artisanal mining is already taking place in the area you want to acquire — which is very common in Africa — it will almost always mean future friction with locals, even if agreements have already been reached with chiefs, kings, or local authorities.

If local miners are already operating, it means an informal or semi-formal ecosystem already exists: miners themselves, suppliers of goods and services, concentrate buyers, security, police, and local officials. All of this is controlled by someone in one form or another.

Then comes water.

Water is essential for washing and processing gold. Rivers become polluted during gold washing. When local artisanal miners do it, authorities view it as a local problem — particularly for farmers downstream who rely on the same water.

But once you arrive, expectations change. Different standards will apply to you. When selecting a lease, you must evaluate not only water availability, but also farms, villages, and grazing land downstream.

In any serious project, you will need water circulation, filtration, and reservoirs or settling ponds. Without this, a proper operation cannot function.

You must also understand water levels during the dry season. Sometimes a river looks powerful during the rainy season but becomes almost dry later, leaving insufficient water for your equipment and processing technology.

Land and mineral resources formally belong to the state and federal authorities, but in practice traditional local leadership — chiefs, kings, elders — plays a major role. Your paperwork may be processed through ministries and federal agencies, but consensus from local traditional authorities is still necessary.

And that is not free.

It may involve one-time payments, annual or monthly contributions, or goodwill projects for the local community: a water borehole, a school bus, a church building, assistance to a school or medical clinic.

Local chiefs or kings may also ask for equity participation. Personally, I would limit this to 10–15 percent — and only if local authorities actually contribute something meaningful, such as local security, political support on the ground, or assistance in dealing with local communities.

But even if local security is provided, you will still need your own security — including protection from the local security itself.

Local security may help manage conflicts with artisanal miners, for whom gold washing is often the only way to feed their families. These people will not simply leave because you show legal documents proving you control the land.

Local chiefs and security usually sympathize with artisanal miners because they are often from the same or neighboring villages. That is why you need your own local negotiators — people who can work alongside you to speak with miners, explain the situation, negotiate, and reduce tensions.

Once you have the lease, geology, and a mining plan prepared by mining engineers and adapted to the local terrain, you can bring in equipment, clear customs, transport machinery to site, and begin installation.

I would strongly recommend starting with a small pilot rather than immediately bringing large-scale equipment. First, test everything in practice: gold grades, equipment performance, water supply, logistics, local behavior, government relations, security, and real production output.

Only after this should you expand and bring in larger equipment.

After your first production batch, even if small, test the entire export and sales chain — whether to Dubai or elsewhere. From my experience with columbite, sometimes surprisingly good buyers can also be found inside the country.

Domestic sales usually mean lower prices, but also lower logistics costs, lower risks, and faster cash turnover.

Everything must be studied, verified, and tested on the ground.

And most importantly — do not rush.
